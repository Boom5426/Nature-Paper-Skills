# Editor-First Front Matter

Use this reference when drafting or revising the title, abstract, cover letter, or top-level manuscript logic for journal submission.

## Core Reader Questions

Make the manuscript answer these questions in order:

1. Why does this study need to be done?
2. What did you do?
3. What did you find?
4. How does this study advance the field?

If the title, abstract, introduction, Results, and discussion answer different versions of these questions, the paper will read as fragmented even if each section is locally strong.

## Title Rules

The title should be a concise summary of what is most important.

Include:
- the central finding, contribution, or research object
- the keywords readers and indexers will actually search for
- only the minimum context needed to identify the work

Prefer:
- about 20 words or fewer
- a direct statement over a setup phrase
- concrete nouns over abstract promotional language

Avoid:
- question-form titles
- method-heavy titles unless the method itself is the main contribution
- abbreviations that a broad reader may not recognize

Quick check:
- Can a reader tell what the paper is mainly about after one pass?
- Does the title foreground the finding rather than the workflow?
- Would the right audience search for the words you used?

## Top-Level Logic

Keep the manuscript on one visible logic chain:

1. Introduce the topic.
2. Summarize the relevant published work.
3. Name the unresolved problem or gap.
4. State the objective.
5. Explain the methodology.
6. Present the results and figures.
7. Summarize the main findings.
8. Interpret those findings.
9. State the implication for the field.

Do not let the introduction, methods, Results, and discussion behave like separate documents.

## Abstract Rules

The abstract is the reader's first pass over the paper. It should let a busy editor, reviewer, or field reader decide quickly whether the paper matters.

Make sure the abstract covers:
- aims and why the topic matters
- the main result or results
- the conclusion and why it is relevant
- clear, direct prose

Force the abstract to answer the four core reader questions:

1. Why did the study need to be done?
   - introduce the topic and the specific problem
2. What did you do?
   - state the aim and the method briefly
3. What did you find?
   - report the key result, not a placeholder like "results are discussed"
4. How will the study advance the field?
   - state the conclusion and implication

Common failure modes:
- spending too many words on generic background
- reporting only that differences were significant without saying what changed
- ending with a weak conclusion that does not say why the work matters

## Cover Letter Rules

The cover letter should help the editor judge significance, journal fit, readership fit, and writing clarity without forcing them to infer the case from the manuscript alone.

Target length:
- about one page

Include:

1. Manuscript identification
   - manuscript title
   - article type
2. Why the study matters
   - brief background
   - the research problem and objective
3. What you found
   - study design in one compact line when useful
   - one or two key findings only
4. Why the paper fits the journal
   - main conclusion
   - why the readership should care
5. Additional submission information
   - reviewer include/exclude notes if requested
   - ethics, originality, conflict-of-interest, and related declarations as needed

Avoid:
- repeating the abstract paragraph-by-paragraph
- listing every result
- making a generic "this work will interest readers" claim without saying which readers and why

## Final Front-Door Check

Before submission, verify that:
- the title, abstract, and cover letter all foreground the same main contribution
- the abstract is not stronger than the Results
- the journal-fit claim in the cover letter matches the actual evidence package
